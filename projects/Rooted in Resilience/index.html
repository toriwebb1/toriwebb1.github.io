Memoir · Photography Rooted in Resilience

A deeply personal memoir tracing my long journey of growing up with an eating disorder — and the quiet resilience it took to find my own voice again.

For years I carried this struggle in silence, listening to a voice that told me I wasn't enough. This is my way of speaking back to it — telling the story honestly, in words and photographs, so the younger me finally hears that she was always worthy.

Voices of Healing and Growth The last time I viewed food as just food alone was in grade school. Since then, my life has been shaped by the shadow of an eating disorder, a relentless presence I've fought to recover from, even when its echoes sometimes still linger. Looking back at food from when I was a kid is nostalgic because it reminds me of a time when eating was simple and joyful, before it became tangled in the struggles I later faced. As my body started to grow, a cruel persistent voice began to take over my mind. This voice whispered lies and brutal comments telling me I wasn't enough. Telling me I had to restrict myself in order to stay small, telling me that my weight equaled my approval, telling me that being skinny reflected my worth. The worst part about this voice was that I believed it, I fully clung onto it. I restricted myself endlessly for years, chasing an impossible ideal, only to feel more insecure the longer I chose this voice over my own. This voice played a constant, tortuous back and forth in my mind, a game I played but couldn't escape. Some days, I looked in the mirror and thought I was too skinny and frail to be understood. While other days, I felt that I was too fat because my jeans from years before didn't fit the same way. Food had become an obsession, a constant lingering thought, whether it was about how much I starve myself that day or if I was "allowed" to eat what I needed that day. I knew exactly what foods had the most calories and what foods would make me gain weight. Even if it was healthy food, there was stress in every bite. My body image also became an obsession as it was directly related to food. I started to weigh myself constantly and try to make my clothes from my youth fit me right. There was anxiety in every glance at my reflection. I felt I had to prove I was skinny… But to who? And why? Questions I still long to answer. And I kept it mainly to myself. My eating disorder was my secret, hidden from most of my friends, family, and even myself at times. I skipped lunch in high school, pretending I was busy with projects, all to avoid the truth of sitting with food I didn't want to eat. I hated going out to eat with friends and family because I was forced to face my reality. I was ashamed of my insecurity, ashamed of how much I compared myself to others, how much I craved their approval. There were times that were unbearable; days when I felt I couldn't eat, where a single apple felt like too much. And then, there were days when hunger overwhelmed me, when I binged, feeling powerless to stop. This cycle of restricting, binging, recovering, and relapsing, became my normal for over five years. Recovery was a process that took years as it was again a constant back and forth. Even now, after my recovery, I cannot see food the way I once did as a child sitting carefree with friends at the lunch table. That cruel voice hasn't fully disappeared and I don't think it ever will. But I've learned to find my own voice that is louder and stronger. I've learned to accept the love others give me, and truly embrace it. I've found the courage to speak, to share, to reflect. This is for Tori, the beautiful younger me. These are the voices I wish I could have said to her, voices I wish she had heard. These are the voices that tells her she is loved, and that she is already worthy.
